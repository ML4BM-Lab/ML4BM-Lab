Current and past research projects in machine learning for biomedicine.

Transcriptional Mechanisms & Drug Repurposing via Machine Learning

We hypothesize that aberrant activity of transcription factors (TFs) generates aberrant transcriptional profiles that drive advanced cancer and treatment resistance. We develop computational methods — including SparseGMM, TraRe, NetActivity, and GeNNius — to identify dysregulated TFs and biological processes, and repurpose known drugs to overcome resistance. TraRe was applied to advanced prostate cancer to identify abrogated TFs associated with drug response; NetActivity translates gene expression into gene set activity scores via interpretable autoencoders; GeNNius predicts drug-target interactions using Graph Neural Networks at state-of-the-art performance.

Funding: H2020 Marie Skłodowska-Curie IF (No. 898356), US DoD-CDMRP (W81XWH-20-1-0262), Spanish Ministry of Science and Innovation (RYC2021-033127-I, TED2021-131300B-I00, PID2023-151980OB-I00), joint ISCIII/NIH project (AC23_2/00016).

Computational Models for Single-Cell Transcriptional Machinery

Single-cell RNA-seq enables fine-grained study of cellular composition and regulatory dynamics. We develop tools for automated cell-type annotation (JIND), gene regulatory network inference at single-cell resolution (SimiC), and lncRNA quantification from single-cell data (ELATUS). These have been applied to Multiple Myeloma CAR T cell studies, del(5q) myelodysplastic syndromes, liver regeneration, and Triple-Negative Breast Cancer, uncovering molecular mechanisms missed by bulk approaches.

Funding: La Caixa Health Research Project (HR24-01000), Palatchi Foundation, Government of Navarra (DIAMANTE, GRANATE, BLANCA projects).

Efficient Storage & Representation of Genomic Data

The genomics field generates enormous and ever-growing volumes of heterogeneous data. Our research addresses challenges in the compression, storage, exchange, and analysis of genomic sequencing data. We have contributed to the MPEG-G standard (ISO/IEC 23092) — the first open international standard for genomic data compression — and developed tools including SPRING, CALQ, GABAC, and Genie to make genomic data more accessible and efficient to handle.

Funding: Spanish Ministry of Science and Innovation (PID2020-114394RA-C33), industrial partnership with Philips Healthcare.
